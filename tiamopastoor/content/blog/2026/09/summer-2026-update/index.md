Welcome to my update article for Summer 2026. It is an overview of the work I've done, projects I've finished, and any other interesting developments during the months of July, August, and September 2026. Let's see how it went!

The [previous update article](/blog/2026/06/spring-2026-update/) explained how I really struggle to earn an income from my work. It also explained all the different things I tried and considered, and how the conclusion was still that I was already doing the right things. Launching my [online store](https://thewaywardmelody.com), the kinds of products I can make for it, it's all designed to fit _me_ as well as possible, while maximizing the chance of earning money.

And, so, that article basically summed up everything else I _could_ be doing ... and how I was _not_ going to do that. Stick to the plan, slowly continue work on the (educational) online store, try and make it work.

However ...

## Professional Work

The past year or so I've consciously **tried to enter some of the creative fields in which I work "professionally"**. It means I stop self-publishing everything, stop throwing games online for free, and start using the typical "professional" ways to get paid for professional work.

I released books with actual publishers. I released a video game on Steam.

And it showed me that it's **just not going to work**.

I have repeatedly talked about how I basically lost my motivation---my ability to enjoy anything at all---when I was forced to go to university. That's ~12 years ago now. I have tried and tried since then, but I have never actually felt like living or doing anything since that moment. All the books I've written have been a fucking nightmare from start to finish, because I had zero motivation or enjoyment from start to finish. The same for my video games, my music recordings, etcetera. That's why I try to blast through all projects and finish them within two weeks, because that's how much discipline I can muster.

But now I was "forced" to be professional and work longer on a project. A publisher generally reserves 6-12 months to have you edit your book (multiple times) before release. Steam has loads of rules and restrictions, and you have to pass several rounds of review that prove your game is what it claims to be. 

And two things happened.

* **The things I made became way better.** More polished, more error-free, more _professional_. I completely understand _why_ these processes exist, because they work. I've now seen the difference between my old work and the heavily revised, edited, professionally made work. It's the difference between something people will pay money for---and something for which they don't.
* **My mental and physical health completely crashed.** When you have no will to do anything, and you get no reward or enjoyment out of anything, then working on the same thing and executing someone else's instructions for months and months is just too much. 

After finishing work on my book (sold by a Dutch publisher), I could not do _anything_ for weeks. All the discipline and good habits in the world can't make up for a soul that feels crushed and drained. Ever since finishing the final edit on that book, I **haven't written a single thing again**. Normally I write one book a month, now I haven't written in half a year for the first time in forever.

After releasing my Steam game, I could not do _anything_ for weeks. I literally wandered around aimlessly for weeks because my body just wanted to die, but I know that's not good for you, so I tried to stay active and keep doing ... something. In practice, I watched a few episodes from different shows, tried to play video games without success, and otherwise just spend hours doing nothing and sitting alone with my thoughts. I have loads of great game ideas to put on Steam, but now I feel like **I never even want to touch a computer ever again**.

## "Just Keep Trying Things"

The common advice to people with burn-out or depression is to "keep doing things". To not overwork yourself, of course, but also not to fall completely still. And so I did. I did that for 15 years.

But these handful of experiences with putting a professional, paid product into the world showed me that it's just _not going to work_ as long as I'm still depressed. I'm not going to be able to work on projects for long enough if I don't get at least a spark of enjoyment back. I'm not going to be able to _sustainably_ do this if I have to recover for a month after each project. Nothing I make will ever be _good enough_ until I'm able to actually enjoy working on it for at least a few months. I've seen the proof now of what it takes---and the proof that you _can't_ earn an income on mediocre rushed projects that you hated to work on from the start.

To be clear, this is not about "working too hard". Those book edits took one or two hours a week at most. When I worked on that Steam game, I gave myself twice the amount of time I thought I needed, and it allowed me to take days off and take lots of breaks. I know how to take care of my physical health, I know how to pace myself (and projects). In all possible ways you could define "working too hard", I don't think I did.

It's about the fact that every single second I have to forcefully convince myself to do _anything_. I should feel proud or something about releasing a professional video game, I should feel happy that a publisher thought my work was good enough to publish the book, but I _don't_. Not even a single bit. Not secretly. I'm not humble or perfectionist, I just don't fucking care about anything and I haven't for almost 15 years.

And so, as the summer started, I wondered what to do. 

Mind you, I had fallen completely still for 2+ weeks at this point. Released my game, never looked back, never did anything else again. I _tried_ to pick a new idea, I started on some prototypes for like 5 minutes before feeling so sick I couldn't even stand and move my mouse anymore. I even sent a list of all my ideas to family members so they could pick the ones they liked the most. They narrowed it down to ~10 ideas, but they happened to pick the largest and most difficult ones, so I still couldn't find the will to start on one of those.

{{% remark %}}
By the way, this heavily curated list with a one-sentence summary of video game ideas ... was still 7 pages long. My overly active/creative brain is the _second_ problem in this situation. It invents 20 ideas a day, of which 19 are rubbish and 1 or 2 good, but that's still 10 new good ideas a week, and it just makes it impossible to pick anything and focus on anything.
{{% /remark %}}

I know myself. I know that, if I keep waiting for a few more weeks, I'll eventually pick something and start creating again. But now I know that whatever I create will not be professional and will thus not earn an income---unless I torture myself again, which I don't feel like doing.

## Getting Help

The first thing I did was obviously to check in with the hospital. I am confirmed to be on the waiting list for some type of aid now. At the same time, they've said they "won't treat ADHD, only the depression" and summarized my problems as "you lack motivation to do what you need to do", which is ... meh. 

I've been very clear about what I think and feel for all those 15 years, and I haven't met a single person who actually listens and doesn't make up their own reality in which I'm supposedly lazy or anxious. 

How the fuck can you look at someone who's created over one hundred books, and games, and more, and conclude they are lazy? :p 

My general practitioner keeps forgetting this fact too. Every conversation starts with him assuming I lie in bed all day and I should just "move out and get a job", and then I correct him, and then he's like "well, we don't know the cause of your problems, so just learn to live with it".

Every single time he also assumes that it's _me_ who stops treatment or doesn't put any effort into it. Then I correct him by saying it has always been _the psychologist_ who broke off treatment while I was doing all they asked of me---which he can verify to know I'm speaking the truth---and then he just looks awkward and changes the subject.

Great. Great help. 

I just don't know why people keep doing this. I'm a writer, an engineer of mathematics, and someone who long ago decided they were never going to lie again. I can communicate very clearly, in whatever way is required, and be almost obsessed with making sure I tell the objective truth (without subconscious embellishment or framing).

And people just keep pretending I said the opposite of what I said, and assume their own reality about who I really am and what my problems really are. And then---surprise surprise---they treat me for their own assumed diagnosis and it doesn't work at all!

It's honestly a bigger issue. I feel like you need to put on a show, like really _convince_ the hospital/general practitioner how sick you are and how much you are in pain, before they actually take you seriously.

_Because_ I've always worked so hard, tried things, took care of my health as much as I could, I've just never been taken seriously by anyone. Not even my own parents. After 15 years of saying "every single second at university made me suicidal", my mother still regularly recommends that I just "go and study something else then". After 15 years of saying that I don't get enjoyment out of anything, every psychologist still writes in their report that I love to do creative stuff and should just do more of it to be happy. Most of all, everyone just says I "look like a fit young man that exercises enough", and decides that means there are no issues at all and they won't look into my case any more.

It's fucking baffling.

I am 100% certain that if I just _gave up_ long ago, if I actually _stayed in bed all day_ and stopped taking care of my health, if I became grumpy and hostile to everyone, they would have actually taken me seriously and treated me. If I broke down crying all the time and told the doctor I'd commit suicide if they didn't help me, then they would have actually taken me seriously.

But I won't put on a show (remember my principle about always being honest and authentic?) and I won't put myself into even more pain (by stopping exercise) just to convince a stranger.

So, anyway, I'm on a waiting list and trying to get medication, but it will probably be the same old story of not actually getting help at all.

## What Else?

As stated, I don't believe (anymore) that just working on the next project and the next will actually lead to a sustainable income. I just don't have the physical and mental health to torture myself through a "professional" project (including all the steps and months I now know that it takes to make one).

I decided that I just had to break entirely with that idea and go do something else. 

I've tried to become a better and better artist, make more and more projects, for quite a long time now. It's been endless long days of working hard on the next thing, mostly behind my stand-up desk in my tiny bedroom, with some rare days of going out or doing something else. (Of course I go outside each day to run 5 miles, and I take breaks to eat lunch and read newspapers and stuff, but I still consider that part of the same repetitive daily home routine.) 

And I did that because creativity just happens automatically (so what I do is more like _not stopping it_). And because it's the one thing I remember that _used_ to give me enjoyment and purpose long ago (before our amazing system of education broke me down completely).

Maybe I should just get a random job, move somewhere else, become a ranger or something.

Maybe I should invent random reasons to overthrow the repetitive daily programming and forcefully keep myself away from rushing through more creative projects.
